Chapter 1 – Matrices

An aside on the determinant

Ah yes, the study of matrices.

I've always found it interesting that this book starts off batting with matrices and their properties. Most algebra textbooks start with set theory, category theory, or jump straight into books, but Artin has a broader plan.

Matrices end up a central tool of study for the topics that are most interesting in this book: vector spaces over arbitrary fields, symmetries of the plane, compact and differentiable groups, representation theory, etc.

A basis is almost always chosen; this isn't as bad as it seems if you're a coordinate-free purist. While you've cached in a choice at the price of more elements to juggle around, you have gain a concrete method for computing elementary operations and determinants, but perhaps most importantly, you get to visualize whats happening.

In the second edition of this book (which is the hard-copy I have), Artin doesn't talk much about the geometric effects of a matrix on the underlying Rn\mathbb R^nRn vector space. In the third edition, he finally adds commentary on the meaning of the determinant, providing a key fact in the understanding of matrices: the determinant measures the expansion or contraction of a unit-cube after its pushed through a matrix, with sign indicating orientiation. It doesn't get more important than that –- this is a fact that, on my first encounter, I just took to be a "cute" side effect of the definitions. But this fact extends far further than the insulated study of matrices, and underlies one of my favorite mathematical objects: the alternating tensors.

The alternating tensors give you volumes of projections. In more words, if ψ(v1,⋯ ,vk)\psi(v_1,\cdots,v_k)ψ(v1​,⋯,vk​) is an alternating tensor, then ψ\psiψ measures the resulting volume from adding up different projections of the kkk-dimensional parallelopiped formed by (v1,⋯ ,vk)(v_1,\cdots,v_k)(v1​,⋯,vk​). In the case of ψ(v1,⋯ ,vk)=det⁡([v1,⋯ ,vk])\psi(v_1,\cdots,v_k) = \det([v_1,\cdots,v_k])ψ(v1​,⋯,vk​)=det([v1​,⋯,vk​]), this gives precisely the volume of the parallelopiped formed by the inputted vectors.

Why would you care? For fun, suppose we're working in Rn\mathbb R^nRn and we have an alternating kkk-tensor at every point in space, i.e. a family {ψp}p∈Rn\{\psi_p\}_{p\in \mathbb R^n}{ψp​}p∈Rn​. Taking n=3n=3n=3 and k=2k=2k=2, this is analagous to having a very very tiny parallelogram at every point in space marking how much "k-volume" is being occupied. Why could this be useful? Imagine you have a two-dimensional surface in R3\mathbb R^3R3 and you'd like to know the surface area of the volume. In multivariable calculus, you learn to take a funny integral that looks something like

∫S∣∣∂f∂u×∂f∂v∣∣dudv\int_S || \frac{\partial f}{\partial u} \times \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}||du dv∫S​∣∣∂u∂f​×∂v∂f​∣∣dudv

which you're told is measuring the "surface area" at every local point thanks to the cross-product. Abusing notation and making up math, another way to write this would be to derive a tensor field ψp\psi_pψp​ at every point on the surface which would measure the local deformation the surface applies to a unit square, and then add them up:

∫Sψp\int_S \psi_p∫S​ψp​

where we've dropped the dxdxdx type objects since they're meaningless anyway. If there's a way to derive a tensor field given a surface, and if there's a way to define an integral with respect to these fields, then we can now integrate functions over k-dimensional surfaces.

All that to say: I think starting with matrices, understanding their algebra, and building a geometric intuition for Rn\mathbb R^nRn is good. There are other approaches to the material which are also good, but given that this book will eventually turn to matrix groups like SU(n)SU(n)SU(n), and to be poetic, this seems like a good idea.

1.1.13 – Nilpotent Matrices

Problem: A square matrix AAA is nilpotent if Ak=0A^k = 0Ak=0 for some k>0k > 0k>0. Prove that if AAA is nilpotent, then I+AI+AI+A is invertible. Do this by finding its inverse.

Solution Attempt:

We're looking for the inverse, (I+A)−1(I + A)^{-1}(I+A)−1. Being very tricky and taking a formal power series:

(I+A)−1=1−A+A2−A3+⋯(I+A)^{-1} = 1 - A + A^2 - A^3 + \cdots (I+A)−1=1−A+A2−A3+⋯

note that I have made no attempt to define what equals sign === means in this case, but I'm sure things will work out well; if we find an inverse that works which is a finite sum, we don't have to justify the convergence of the infinite series.

So we calculate. Since Ak=0A^k = 0Ak=0 for some k>0k>0k>0, then An=0A^n = 0An=0 for all n≥k n \geq kn≥k. Therefore we can heuristically see that

(I+A)−1=∑n=0k−1(−1)nAn,(I+A)^{-1} = \sum_{n=0}^{k-1} (-1)^n A^n,(I+A)−1=n=0∑k−1​(−1)nAn,

where we define through analogy A0=IA^0 = IA0=I. Computing by hand,

(I+A)(I+A)−1=(I+A)∑n=0k−1(−1)nAn=∑n=0k−1(−1)nAn+∑n=0k−1(−1)nAn+1=I+(−A+A)+(−A2+A2)+⋯+(−Ak−1+Ak−1)+Ak=I\begin{aligned} (I+A)(I+A)^{-1} &= (I+A)\sum_{n=0}^{k-1}(-1)^n A^n\\ &= \sum_{n=0}^{k-1}(-1)^n A^n + \sum_{n=0}^{k-1}(-1)^n A^{n+1}\\ &= I + (-A + A) + (-A^2 + A^2) + \cdots + (-A^{k-1} + A^{k-1}) + A^k\\ &= I \end{aligned}(I+A)(I+A)−1​=(I+A)n=0∑k−1​(−1)nAn=n=0∑k−1​(−1)nAn+n=0∑k−1​(−1)nAn+1=I+(−A+A)+(−A2+A2)+⋯+(−Ak−1+Ak−1)+Ak=I​

So our candidate inverse ended up working due to in-between terms telescoping and the final AkA^kAk term dying due to the nilpotent property. Since AAA and I+AI+AI+A are square, this inverse is the unique inverse and we're done.

Commentary: From what I can glean without looking into the future, Artin put this problem here to give an appreciation for nilpotent matrices (duh) since they'll come up later (oh! hey thats fun). In the context of differential equations, a linear differential equation

x′=Axx' = Axx′=Ax

admits very different structures depending on the structure of AAA. The formal solution to such a differential equation takes the form of x(t)=exp⁡(At)xx(t) = \exp(At)xx(t)=exp(At)x, where exp⁡(A)\exp(A)exp(A) is defined as a formal power series in AAA and xxx a vector of initial conditions. How do you know if it converges? Well the naïve thing to do is to declare the power series convergent if all entries of the matrix exp⁡(A)\exp(A)exp(A) converge point-wise.

When you plug in a nilpotent matrix into the power series, you end up with a truncated series with all terms above kkk dying off. There are implications on the types of vector fields which can be generated by a differential equation defined through a nilpotent matrix, so dusting off some of this algebra was fun.

I first learned about this in Hirsch and Smale's Differential Equations, Dynamical Systems, and Linear Algebra, which is a much older version of a book under a very similar name. The study of linear differential equations is central to the theory, since, as usual, problems of a local character end up being problems in linear algebra.
