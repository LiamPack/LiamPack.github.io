Linear Algebra; Finite-Dimensional Vector Spaces

tags: algebra, math, notes

last updated: 2022-11-22

Algebra is the backbone of mathematical description, and (finite-dimensional) linear algebra is possibly the most important tool in the tool-belt: whats better than turning a complicated problem into a problem of counting dimension?

Vector spaces are a marriage of a field and a set, FFF and VVV, where we have an operation "addition" on VVV, v1+Vv2∈Vv_1 +_V v_2 \in Vv1​+V​v2​∈V. These objects are connected through distributivity, (a+Fb)v=av+Vbc(a +_F b)v = av +_V bc(a+F​b)v=av+V​bc and a(v1+Vv2)=av1+Vav2a(v_1 +_V v_2) = av_1 +_V av_2a(v1​+V​v2​)=av1​+V​av2​. Once we have this, we can drop the subscripts on +++ without harm. Once you define the connection, the rest follows by taking all possible linear combinations and throwing them into a bag (VVV). A common theme is that we start with a smaller bag, V′V'V′ and then enlarge it by taking formal linear combinations av1+bv2av_1 + bv_2av1​+bv2​ and "add it" to V′V'V′. Then the total accumulation of these new elements will be called VVV, and by construction satisfies the properties of a vector space (similar in spirit to a sub-basis or pre-basis in topology, pre-Hilbert space, etc.)

By taking specifically a field FFF to be our coefficients, we get a surprising amount of structure for free since every non-zero element of FFF is a unit (has a multiplicative inverse).

Problems and commentary will be pulling from:

  • Artin's Algebra

  • Halmos's Finite-Dimensional Vector Spaces

While Artin prioritizes computations and intuition from choosing a basis (an isomorphism to a column vector space FnF^nFn), Halmos focuses on remaining in the abstract language of linear maps (no choice of isomorphism to coordinates in FnF^nFn).

Both approaches have advantages: Artin's computational approach pays dividends in later chapters when the matrix exponential comes to the forefront, allowing e.g. Jordan canonical forms to shine, which then comes back in the discussion of compact topological groups, Lie algebras, and Lie groups. Also concrete symmetries of the plane which are related and fun.

Halmos's approach has the advantage of using language and techniques consistent with the fields of functional analysis and Hilbert space theory, where the primary difference is that your underlying vector space may be infinite-dimensional.

Some Thoughts on Coordinates and Bases

What is a Basis

Basis is a term that actually pops up relatively frequently in mathematics. The least committal description I can come up with is: a basis is a bag of elements which, when you mix them according to some operation, you can generate any element you want. In linear algebra, this means taking linear combinations of vectors; in topology, this means taking countable unions and finite intersections of open sets; in measure theory, this means taking countable unions and intersections of measurable sets; and so forth.

What's important about a basis is that, once you've chosen a particular basis (some call this "using up your degree of freedom," or "expending your choice"), you can start computing in a more natural way: If you write your basis of a vector space VVV as a tuple of vectors, (v1,⋯ ,vn)(v_1, \cdots, v_n)(v1​,⋯,vn​), there is a natural map B:Fn−>V\mathbf{B}: F^n -> VB:Fn−>V which sends X→BXX \to \mathbf{B}XX→BX , where B\mathbf{B}B is the matrix whose columns consist of the basis elements v1⋯vnv_1 \cdots v_nv1​⋯vn​. Now you can do whatever computation you'd like "row-wise" with the field FFF, and since the basis defines an isomorphism, any results you find will hold in the general case.

This operation is especially powerful (and very frequent!) in something like general relativity. You may have seen Einstein's field equations in their famous form (not including cosmological constant):

Rμν−12Rgμν∝TμνR_{\mu\nu} - \frac12 R g_{\mu\nu} \propto T_{\mu\nu}Rμν​−21​Rgμν​∝Tμν​

This is great: you can compute the evolution of mass and gravity so long as you know some initial conditions on the metric gμνg_{\mu\nu}gμν​ and the stress-energy tensor TμνT_{\mu\nu}Tμν​. A less commonly taught form of the field equations (if you're in undergrad, or taking a first grad course) is in "coordinate free" form:

G∝T\mathbf{G} \propto \mathbf{T}G∝T

This form has advantages: there is a clarity in withholding a choice of coordinates, since the meaning of indices isn't required to understand the statement. So long as you're clear on the definition of a tensor being a multilinear map on copies of VVV and V∗V^*V∗, its a difficult equation to forget (as opposed to index juggling). The downside is, of course, you'll have a hard time calculating anything until you pick a basis.

An aside on the determinant

I've always found it interesting that Artin's Algebra starts off batting with matrices and their properties. Lots of abstract algebra textbooks start with set theory, category theory, or jump straight into groups, but Artin has a broader plan.

Matrices end up a central tool of study for the topics that form a large chunk of the content in this book: vector spaces over arbitrary fields, symmetries of the plane, compact and differentiable groups, representation theory, free modules, Galois theory, etc.

So a basis is almost always chosen in Artin; this isn't as bad as it seems (unless you're a coordinate-free purist). While you cashed in a choice at the price of more elements to juggle around, you gained a concrete method for computing elementary operations and determinants, and perhaps most importantly, you get to visualize whats happening.

In the second edition of Artin (which is the hard-copy I have), there isn't much talk much about the geometric effects of a matrix on the underlying Rn\mathbb R^nRn vector space. In the third edition, he finally adds commentary on the meaning of the determinant, providing a key fact in the understanding of matrices: the determinant measures the expansion or contraction of a unit-cube after its pushed through a matrix, with sign indicating orientiation. It doesn't get more important than that –- this is a fact that, on my first encounter, I just took to be a "cute" side effect of the definitions. But this fact extends far further than the insulated study of matrices, and underlies one of my favorite mathematical objects: the alternating tensors.

The alternating tensors give you volumes of projections. In more words, if ψ(v1,⋯ ,vk)\psi(v_1,\cdots,v_k)ψ(v1​,⋯,vk​) is an alternating tensor, then ψ\psiψ measures the resulting volume from adding up different projections of the kkk-dimensional parallelopiped formed by (v1,⋯ ,vk)(v_1,\cdots,v_k)(v1​,⋯,vk​). In the case of ψ(v1,⋯ ,vk)=det⁡([v1,⋯ ,vk])\psi(v_1,\cdots,v_k) = \det([v_1,\cdots,v_k])ψ(v1​,⋯,vk​)=det([v1​,⋯,vk​]), this gives precisely the volume of the parallelopiped formed by the inputted vectors.

Why would you care? For fun, suppose we're working in Rn\mathbb R^nRn and we have an alternating kkk-tensor at every point in space, i.e. a family {ψp}p∈Rn\{\psi_p\}_{p\in \mathbb R^n}{ψp​}p∈Rn​. Taking n=3n=3n=3 and k=2k=2k=2, this is analagous to having a very very tiny parallelogram at every point in space marking how much "k-volume" is being occupied in a particular orientation. Why could this be useful? Imagine you have a two-dimensional surface in R3\mathbb R^3R3 and you'd like to know the surface area of the volume. In multivariable calculus, you learn to take a funny integral that looks something like

∫S∣∣∂f∂u×∂f∂v∣∣dudv\int_S || \frac{\partial f}{\partial u} \times \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}||du dv∫S​∣∣∂u∂f​×∂v∂f​∣∣dudv

which you're told is measuring the "surface area" at every local point thanks to the cross-product. Abusing notation and making up math, another way to write this would be to derive a tensor field ψp\psi_pψp​ at every point on the surface which would measure the local deformation the surface applies to a unit square, and then add them up:

∫Sψp\int_S \psi_p∫S​ψp​

where we've dropped the dxdxdx type objects since they're meaningless anyway. If there's a way to derive a tensor field given a surface, and if there's a way to define an integral with respect to these fields, then we can now integrate functions over k-dimensional surfaces.

All that to say: I think starting with matrices, understanding their algebra, and building a geometric intuition for Rn\mathbb R^nRn is good. There are other approaches to the material which are also good, but given that this book will eventually turn to matrix groups like SU(n)SU(n)SU(n), and to be poetic, this seems like a good idea.

Artin 1.1.13; Nilpotent Matrices

Problem: A square matrix AAA is nilpotent if Ak=0A^k = 0Ak=0 for some k>0k > 0k>0. Prove that if AAA is nilpotent, then I+AI+AI+A is invertible. Do this by finding its inverse.

Solution Attempt:

We're looking for the inverse, (I+A)−1(I + A)^{-1}(I+A)−1. Being very tricky and taking a formal power series:

(I+A)−1=1−A+A2−A3+⋯(I+A)^{-1} = 1 - A + A^2 - A^3 + \cdots (I+A)−1=1−A+A2−A3+⋯

note that I have made no attempt to define what equals sign === means in this case, but I'm sure things will work out well; if we find an inverse that works which is a finite sum, we don't have to justify the convergence of the infinite series.

So we calculate. Since Ak=0A^k = 0Ak=0 for some k>0k>0k>0, then An=0A^n = 0An=0 for all n≥k n \geq kn≥k. Therefore we can heuristically see that

So our candidate inverse ended up working due to in-between terms telescoping and the final AkA^kAk term dying due to the nilpotent property. Since AAA and I+AI+AI+A are square, this inverse is the unique inverse and we're done.

Commentary: From what I can glean without looking into the future, Artin put this problem here to give an appreciation for nilpotent matrices (duh) since they'll come up later (oh! hey thats fun). In the context of differential equations, a linear differential equation

x′=Axx' = Axx′=Ax

admits very different structures depending on the structure of AAA. The formal solution to such a differential equation takes the form of x(t)=exp⁡(At)xx(t) = \exp(At)xx(t)=exp(At)x, where exp⁡(A)\exp(A)exp(A) is first defined as a formal power series in AAA and xxx a vector of initial conditions. You need some elbow grease to make sure the power series exp⁡(A)\exp(A)exp(A) "converges" to something, but for that you need a nice topology on FFF, so I digress.

When you plug in a nilpotent matrix into the power series, you end up with a truncated series with all terms above kkk dying off. There are implications on the types of vector fields which can be generated by a differential equation defined through a nilpotent matrix, so dusting off some of this algebra was fun. To get a complete understanding of their importance, we'll have to wait until Jordan forms, where pieces of some "typical" nilpotent matrices are associated to generalized eigenspaces with dimension ≥2\geq 2≥2, i.e., pieces of your linear operator that have non-distinct eigenvalues.

I first learned about this stuff in Hirsch and Smale's Differential Equations, Dynamical Systems, and Linear Algebra, which is a much older version of a book under a very similar name. The study of linear differential equations is central to the theory of differential equations, since, as usual, a nonlinear equation x′=f(x)x' = f(x)x′=f(x) reduces to a linear-type equation x′=Df(x0)xx' = Df(x_0)xx′=Df(x0​)x, so long as you stay in a tight neighborhood of x0x_0x0​.
